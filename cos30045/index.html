Appliance Energy Consumption

Understanding energy use in Australian households — helping you save money and reduce your carbon footprint.

Why Energy Efficiency Matters

Household appliances account for a significant portion of Australia's total energy consumption. With electricity prices among the highest in the world, understanding how much energy your appliances consume is a critical step toward reducing household bills and environmental impact.

The Australian Government's Energy Rating program provides star-rated labels on appliances, making it easier for consumers to compare the running costs of different products before purchasing.

Top Energy-Consuming Appliances

❄️

Air Conditioners

Heating and cooling accounts for nearly 40% of home energy use. A 5-star rated reverse-cycle unit can cut costs significantly compared to older models.

🫙

Refrigerators

Fridges run 24/7, making efficiency ratings crucial. Modern inverter-driven compressors use up to 40% less energy than models from 10 years ago.

📺

Televisions

Large screen TVs can consume between 50–400 watts. LED and OLED technologies are considerably more efficient than older plasma screens.

🧺

Washing Machines

Front-loaders typically use 40–70% less water and energy than top-loaders. Cold water wash cycles further reduce energy costs.

💧

Dishwashers

Modern dishwashers with eco-modes can use as little as 9 litres of water per cycle, compared to up to 100 litres for hand-washing a full load.

☀️

Solar & Storage

Pairing energy-efficient appliances with rooftop solar and battery storage is increasingly popular, with over 3 million Australian homes having solar panels.

Energy Saving Tips

Small behavioural changes combined with efficient appliances can lead to meaningful reductions in energy bills:

  • Set your refrigerator to 3–4°C and freezer to -15 to -18°C.
  • Use the economy or eco mode on dishwashers and washing machines.
  • Switch off appliances at the power point rather than leaving on standby.
  • Set your air conditioner to 24°C in summer — each degree lower increases running costs by ~10%.
  • Look for the highest star rating when replacing old appliances.
  • Wash clothes in cold water and air-dry when possible.

Frequently Asked Questions

The Energy Rating label is a government-backed scheme that appears on many household appliances sold in Australia and New Zealand. It shows a star rating (more stars = more efficient) and an annual energy consumption figure in kilowatt-hours (kWh), allowing you to compare the running costs of different models before buying.

In a typical Australian home, the biggest energy users are heating and cooling systems (around 40%), water heating (around 25%), and refrigeration (around 7%). Other significant contributors include lighting, ovens, and entertainment equipment such as televisions and gaming consoles.

Savings vary depending on the appliance type, usage patterns, and the efficiency difference between the old and new model. On average, upgrading from a 2-star to a 5-star refrigerator can save approximately $80–$120 per year. Over the appliance's lifetime of 10–15 years, total savings can exceed the upfront cost premium of choosing a higher-rated model.

Standby power (sometimes called "phantom load" or "vampire power") refers to the electricity consumed by appliances when they are switched off but still plugged in. Common culprits include televisions, set-top boxes, gaming consoles, and phone chargers. In Australia, standby power can account for up to 10% of a household's total electricity bill. Switching appliances off at the power point eliminates standby consumption entirely.

Under a TOU plan, electricity costs more during peak demand periods (typically weekday evenings) and less during off-peak periods (late night and weekends). Running high-consumption appliances such as dishwashers, washing machines, and pool pumps during off-peak hours can reduce your electricity bill. Check with your retailer to see if you are on a TOU plan.

The Australian Government Energy Rating website (energyrating.gov.au) provides a comprehensive product database, running cost calculators, and detailed guidance for consumers. The Department of Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water also publishes household energy usage data and tips for reducing consumption.

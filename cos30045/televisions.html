Television Energy Consumption

Comparing TV technologies, screen sizes, and their impact on your electricity bill.

Overview

Televisions are one of the most commonly purchased household appliances in Australia. With screen sizes growing and Australians spending an average of over 2.5 hours per day watching TV, energy consumption from televisions has become a notable household cost.

The energy a television uses depends on its display technology, screen size, brightness settings, and usage patterns. Understanding these factors helps consumers make informed purchasing decisions.

Display Technologies

💡

LED / LCD

The most common TV technology. LED-backlit LCD panels are energy-efficient and affordable. A typical 55" LED TV consumes 50–100 watts. Suited to bright rooms with varied content.

🎨

OLED

Organic LED panels produce per-pixel light, allowing true blacks and exceptional contrast. A 55" OLED typically uses 80–130 watts. Excellent picture quality but higher purchase price.

✨

QLED

Quantum LED technology enhances LED/LCD brightness and colour accuracy using a quantum dot filter. Energy use is similar to LED, ranging 60–110 watts for a 55" screen, with vibrant HDR performance.

🔴

Plasma (Legacy)

Plasma TVs were widely used until the mid-2010s. A 50" plasma could consume 250–400 watts — 3 to 5 times more energy than a modern equivalent. Very few new plasma TVs are sold today.

Buying Tips

📐

Right-Size Your Screen

Larger screens consume more power. Choose a screen size appropriate for your viewing distance — a 55" TV in a small room wastes energy and offers no visual benefit over a 43" model.

⭐

Compare Energy Ratings

Always check the Energy Rating label. Each additional star can represent up to 30% less energy consumption over the life of the appliance, saving you money every year.

🌙

Use Eco / Cinema Mode

Factory brightness settings are often set high for showroom floors. Switching to Eco or Cinema mode can reduce power consumption by 20–40% without significantly impacting picture quality.

🔌

Switch Off at the Wall

TVs in standby mode can draw 1–5 watts continuously. Over a year, that amounts to 9–44 kWh of wasted electricity. Use a smart power strip or switch off at the wall when not in use.
